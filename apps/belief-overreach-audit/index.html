Faith-gap audit

Belief Overreach Audit

Start with a fair die, where both symmetry and repeated experience support only a one-in-six chance that the next roll will be a six. Then watch what happens when confidence moves beyond that support and begins carrying plans, risks, and life commitments the evidence never licensed. Under this definition, faith adds no new guidance value. It only worsens the decision by authorizing commitments the support line never justified.

A narrow use of “faith”. In this tool, faith means only the portion of confidence that rises above the evidence as you yourself perceive it. Here, faith adds no information, no predictive power, and no success value. The question is not whether God exists, but whether your confidence is outrunning your own support line and making the decision itself worse.
How to use this audit

First, set your confidence about the next roll of a fair die being a six. The die fixes the support at 16.7%. The slider shows whether your belief stays inside that support or walks beyond it.

Next, run a short sequence of rolls. The tool compares what the evidence would warrant, what your confidence plans for, and what the world actually delivers.

Finally, transfer the same structure to religious claims, including which god, if any, to worship. Rate the support you think you really have, then rate how confident you are anyway. The gap is the portion this tool labels faith.

Deductive symmetry ? This is the mathematical argument. If the die is fair, each face has the same probability. Call that probability p. The six faces exhaust all possible outcomes, so p + p + p + p + p + p = 1. That gives 6p = 1, so p = 1/6 = 0.1667 = 16.67%. Under the fairness assumption, the next roll being a six is fixed by symmetry and probability arithmetic, not by hope, intuition, or preference.

No side of a fair die has a privileged route to the next roll.

Inductive experience ? This is the empirical check. If you throw the die n times and get k sixes, the observed rate is k / n. In short runs that rate can swing high or low, but across longer runs it keeps settling near 1/6. Induction does not prove the next throw deductively. It shows repeated experience converging on the same value that symmetry predicts.

Long runs keep settling around one six in every six throws.

Honest credence ? This is the norm for belief. If the support you perceive is 1/6 = 16.67%, then honest credence stops there. On this tool's definition, faith begins when belief rises above that line. For example, if you choose 40%, the measure of faith is 40 - 16.7 = 23.3 points. Core rationality here is simply: belief = perceived support, not belief > perceived support.

If you stop at 16.7%, belief stays inside the support you perceive.

Religious pressure

Why praising faith is intrinsically irrational

Many religions do not merely permit confidence to outrun perceived support. They praise it. They treat trust, surrender, loyalty, or obedience beyond what the believer thinks they can really show as spiritually admirable. They also often recruit motives such as hope, comfort, longing, fear, or the desire for meaning to push confidence upward. Under this audit's definition, that is intrinsically irrational because none of those motives increases the actual degree of evidence the believer perceives.

Rule change

It treats overbelief as a virtue.

Core rationality says confidence should stop at the support line. A religion that praises faith asks the person to cross that line on purpose.

Hope pressure

Hope can motivate belief without supporting it.

Hope for healing, rescue, justice, purpose, reunion, or eternal life can make a higher confidence setting feel attractive. But hope changes what a person wants to be true, not how much support they actually perceive.

Error control

It weakens self-correction.

Once doubt is framed as weakness or disloyalty, evidence loses authority. That makes bad beliefs harder to reduce when the support is thin.

Worship choice

It worsens high-stakes religious decisions.

If several religions or gods compete for allegiance, a method that rewards confidence beyond perceived support makes it more likely that worship, obedience, and identity attach to the wrong object or path.

On this view, the problem is not only that faith sometimes ends badly. The problem is already present in the method itself: any instruction to believe beyond perceived support, even for the sake of hope, is a standing instruction to reason less honestly.
